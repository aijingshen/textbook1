4.2.2. One-compartment Kidney Model#

A 1-compartment model of urea removal during dialysis involves balancing urea generation, removal by the kidneys, and removal by dialysis.

4.2.2.1. Urea Balance in the Body#

The total rate of change of urea concentration in the blood can be described by considering:

  1. Urea Generation: Urea is continuously generated in the body at a constant rate “G”.

  2. Urea Removal by the Kidneys: The kidneys remove urea at a rate proportional to the concentration of urea, which can be modeled as \(k_r C(t)\).

  3. Urea Removal by Dialysis: Similarly, dialysis removes urea at a rate proportional to the concentration, modeled as \(k_d C(t)\).

Thus, the overall rate of change of the amount of urea in the blood can be expressed by the equation:

\[ \frac{d (C(t) V_d)}{dt} = G - k_r C(t) V_d - k_d C(t) V_d \]

The left side represents the rate of change of the total amount of urea in the blood (concentration times volume). The right side represents the generation of urea and the removal by both the kidneys and dialysis.

Where:

  • \(C(t)\): Concentration of urea in the blood at time \(t\)(mg/L)

  • \(V_d\): Volume of distribution for urea (L)

  • \(G\): Urea generation rate (mg/hr)

  • \(k_r\): Renal (kidney) elimination rate constant (1/hr)

  • \(k_d\): Dialysis elimination rate constant (1/hr)

The term \(V_d\) is the volume of distribution, which is assumed to be constant. We can divide through by \(V_d\) to express the rate of change of urea concentration:

\[ \frac{dc(t)}{dt} = \frac{G}{V_d} - (k_r + k_d) C(t) \]

This is a first-order linear differential equation. The general solution to this equation is:

\[ c(t) = \frac{G}{V_d (k_r + k_d)} + \left( C_0 - \frac{G}{V_d (k_r + k_d)} \right) e^{-(k_r + k_d) t} \]

Where:

  • \(c_0\) is the initial concentration of urea at \(t = 0\).

  • The first term, \(\frac{G}{V_d (k_r + k_d)}\), represents the steady-state concentration of urea that will be reached over time if the generation, kidney, and dialysis clearance rates remain constant.

  • The second term represents the decay of the initial concentration \(c_0\) over time. The decay rate depends on the combined elimination rate constant \(k_r + k_d\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Define the function for the urea concentration over time
def urea_concentration(t, G, V_d, k_r, k_d, C_0):
    k_total = k_r + k_d  # combined elimination rate constant
    steady_state_concentration = G / (V_d * k_total)
    return steady_state_concentration + (C_0 - steady_state_concentration) * np.exp(-k_total * t)

# Parameters
G = 20   # Urea generation rate (mg/hr)
V_d = 40  # Volume of distribution (L)
k_r = 0.1  # Renal elimination rate constant (1/hr)
k_d = 0.2  # Dialysis elimination rate constant (1/hr)
C_0 = 25  # Initial concentration of urea (mg/L)

# Time points from 0 to 24 hours
t = np.linspace(0, 24, 500)

# Calculate the urea concentration over time
C_values = urea_concentration(t, G, V_d, k_r, k_d, C_0)

# Plot the graph
plt.plot(t, C_values, label=r'$C(t)=\frac{G}{V_d(k_r+k_d)} + \left( C_0 - \frac{G}{V_d(k_r+k_d)} \right) e^{-(k_r+k_d)t}$')
plt.axhline(y=G / (V_d * (k_r + k_d)), color='r', linestyle='--', label='Steady State Concentration')

# Plot formatting
plt.xlabel('Time (hours)')
plt.ylabel('Urea Concentration (mg/L)')
plt.title('Urea Concentration Over Time During Dialysis')
plt.legend()
plt.ylim(0, None)
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

4.2.2.2. The effect of Generation on steady state#

    k_total = k_r + k_d  # combined elimination rate constant
    steady_state_concentration = G / (V_d * k_total)
    return steady_state_concentration + (C_0 - steady_state_concentration) * np.exp(-k_total * t)

# Parameters
V_d = 40  # Volume of distribution (L)
k_r = 0.1  # Renal elimination rate constant (1/hr)
k_d = 0.2  # Dialysis elimination rate constant (1/hr)
C_0 = 25  # Initial concentration of urea (mg/L)

# Time points from 0 to 24 hours
t = np.linspace(0, 24, 500)

# Different values of G to plot
G_values = [0, 50, 100, 200]  # Urea generation rates (mg/hr)

# Plot for each G
for G in G_values:
    C_values = urea_concentration(t, G, V_d, k_r, k_d, C_0)
    plt.plot(t, C_values, label=f'$G = {G}$')

# Add the initial concentration line
plt.axhline(y=C_0, color='gray', linestyle='--', label='Initial Concentration')

# Plot formatting
plt.xlabel('Time (hours)')
plt.ylabel('Urea Concentration (mg/L)')
plt.title('Urea Concentration Over Time for Different Urea Generation Rates')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

4.2.2.3. Steady State solution#

At steady-state, the concentration of urea does not change with time. This means that the rate of change of concentration, \(\frac{dC(t)}{dt}\) is equal to zero:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} 0 = \frac{G}{V_d} - (k_r + k_d) C_{\text{ss}} \\ (k_r + k_d) C_{\text{ss}} = \frac{G}{V_d} \\ C_{\text{ss}} = \frac{G}{V_d (k_r + k_d)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Steady-State Concentration: The steady-state concentration of urea is inversely proportional to the combined clearance rates (kidneys + dialysis). If dialysis is more effective (higher \(k_d\)), the steady-state urea concentration will be lower.

4.2.2.4. No Generation during dialysis#

If we assume that the urea generation rate G is negligible during dialysis (G=0), the equation simplifies because there is no constant influx of urea into the system. The only factors we need to consider are the initial concentration of urea \(C_0\), kidney removal, and dialysis removal. Starting from the general equation:

\[ \frac{dC(t)}{dt} = \frac{G}{V_d} - (k_r + k_d) C(t) \]

If \(G = 0\) (no urea generation), this simplifies to:

\[ \frac{dC(t)}{dt} = -(k_r + k_d) C(t) \]

This is a simple first-order differential equation of the form:

\[ \frac{dC(t)}{dt} = -k_{\text{total}} C(t) \]

where \( k_{\text{total}} = k_r + k_d \) is the combined elimination rate constant.

The solution to this equation is an exponential decay function:

\[ C(t) = C_0 e^{-k_{\text{total}} t} \]

where:

  • \(C_0\) is the initial concentration of urea at \(t = 0\).

  • \(k_{\text{total}} = k_r + k_d\) represents the overall rate at which urea is removed from the body.

  • The concentration \(C(t)\) decays exponentially with time, as no new urea is generated during dialysis.

This equation shows that the concentration decreases exponentially over time, and the decay rate depends on the sum of the kidney and dialysis elimination rates.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Define the function for the urea concentration over time (no generation)
def urea_concentration_no_G(t, k_r, k_d, C_0):
    k_total = k_r + k_d  # combined elimination rate constant
    return C_0 * np.exp(-k_total * t)

# Parameters
k_r = 0.1  # Renal elimination rate constant (1/hr)
k_d = 0.2  # Dialysis elimination rate constant (1/hr)
C_0 = 25  # Initial concentration of urea (mg/L)

# Time points from 0 to 24 hours
t = np.linspace(0, 24, 500)

# Calculate the urea concentration over time
C_values = urea_concentration_no_G(t, k_r, k_d, C_0)

# Plot the graph
plt.plot(t, C_values, label=r'$C(t) = C_0 e^{-(k_r+k_d)t}$')

# Plot formatting
plt.xlabel('Time (hours)')
plt.ylabel('Urea Concentration (mg/L)')
plt.title('Urea Concentration Over Time During Dialysis (No Generation)')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()
