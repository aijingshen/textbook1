Alcohol Compartmental Quiz#

Question: Your classmate borrowed your car for the night. When you see him the next day, he’s obviously hung over, and you’re concerned that he may have driven your vehicle while drunk. With a few discreet questions, you find out that he drank 4 shots of Tequila (!) at 10PM and drove your car home at 2AM the next day. Each shot is approximately 40 mL and the tequila was 80 proof (40% ethanol by volume). If you assume that the shots were drunk very quickly and rapidly absorbed, you may assume that your classmate began with 4 shots worth of alcohol in his central compartment at 10PM. If only you knew his clearance rate, you’d be able to calculate his blood alcohol level at 2AM! Conveniently enough, your classmate’s brother took part in a study at UCSF that tested his alcohol clearance, which was kclearance = 1.82E-4 1/hr. Your classmate weighs 66 kg and your classmate’s brother weighs 70 kg. With the exception of their different sizes, their bodies probably deal with ethanol in a fairly similar fashion. For ethanol, the volume of distribution Vd is 0.80 L/kg of body weight. For the density of ethanol \(0.79 \, \text{g/mL}\).

What is the concentration of ethanol (in mol/L, with 3 significant digits) in your roommate’s blood at 2AM?

What is the correct initial dose of alcohol?#

At time \(t=0, c(t)\) must be \(c_{0}\) (the initial concentration)

We use the first to solve for A.

\[ c_{0}=A e^{0} \]

Which gives us \(\mathrm{A}=c_{0}\) and

\[ c(t)=c_{0} e^{-k t} \]

Now, we solve for a \(C_{0}\) given the parameters.

\[ c_{0} = \frac{4 \, \text{shots} \times 40 \, \text{mL/shot} \times 0.40 \times 0.79 \, \text{g/mL}}{0.80 \, \text{L/kg} \times 66 \, \text{kg}} \]

Solving for \(c_{0}\) gives us a concentration of \(0.95758 \ldots \mathrm{g} / \mathrm{L}\) (accounting for sig figs in the last step).

Rate constant varies#

For an 66 kg patient, the volume of distribution for morphine \(V_{d, \text { Tenquila }}\) will be 52.8 L. We often assume that the clearance rate is proportional to the patient’s surface area.

We can estimate the SA from the body weight

\[ \text{Surface area} = 1.73\left(\frac{\text{Body weight in kg}}{70}\right)^{0.73} \]

If the clearance rate is proportional to the surface area of the patient, then we can estimate k for the classmate’s brother of 70 kg:

\[ \frac{k_{66 \mathrm{~kg}}}{k_{70 \mathrm{~kg}}}=\frac{1.73\left(\frac{66 \mathrm{~kg}}{70}\right)^{0.73}}{1.73\left(\frac{70 \mathrm{~kg}}{70}\right)^{0.73}}=\left(\frac{66}{70}\right)^{0.73}=0.95795587169 \]
\[ k_{66\text{kg}} = 1.7434797 \times 10^{-4}\, \frac{1}{\text{hr}} \]

What is the correct final concentration of alcohol?#

We know two things:

  • at time \(t=0, c(t)\) must be \(c_{0}\) (the initial concentration) of \(0.95758 \ldots \mathrm{g} / \mathrm{L}\)

  • your classmate’s clearance rate is of \(1.7434797 \times 10^{-4}\, \frac{1}{\text{hr}}\)

We use the first to solve for A.

\[ c_{0}=A e^{0} \]

Which gives us \(\mathrm{A}=c_{0}\) and

\[ c(t)=c_{0} e^{-k t} \]

Now, we solve for a \(C_{t}\) by substituting the given values:

\[ c(4) = 0.95758 \, e^{-1.7434797 \times 10^{-4} \cdot 4} \]

Solving for \(c_{4}\) gives us a concentration of \(0.9569124243 \ldots \mathrm{g} / \mathrm{L}\) (accounting for sig figs in the last step).

Calculation of Molar Concentration of Ethanol#

Given the mass concentration of ethanol, \(c_{mass} = 0.9569124243 \, \text{g/L}\), and the molar mass of ethanol, \(Molar \, Mass = 46.068 \, \text{g/mol}\), the molar concentration can be calculated by:

\[ c_{mol} = \frac{0.9569124243 \, \text{g/L}}{46.068 \, \text{g/mol}}={0.0208 \, \text{g/mol}} \]

This yields the final molar concentration of ethanol in the solution.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Define the function for the concentration over time
def concentration(t, c_0, k):
    return c_0 * np.exp(-k * t)

# Parameters
c_0 = 0.95758/46.068 # Initial concentration (mol/L)
k = 1.743E-4  # Elimination rate constant (1/hr)
C_at_4 = 0.95691/46.068

# Time points from 0 to 24 hours
t = np.linspace(0, 100000, 500)

# Calculate the concentration over time
C_values = concentration(t, c_0, k)

# Plot the graph
plt.plot(t, C_values, label=r'$c(t) = c_0 e^{-kt}$')
plt.axvline(x=4, color='red', linestyle='--', label='t = 4 hours')
plt.axhline(y=C_at_4, color='blue', linestyle='--', label=f'c(t=4) = {C_at_4:.3f} mol/L')


# Plot formatting
plt.xlabel('Time (hours)')
plt.ylabel('Concentration (mol/L)')
plt.title('Concentration Decay Over Time')
plt.grid(True)
plt.legend()
plt.show()
