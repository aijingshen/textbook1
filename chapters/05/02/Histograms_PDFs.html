5.16. Example 1: Rolling 2 dice#

Let’s roll two dice and add the values together.

# Function to roll two dice and return their sum
def roll_two_dice():
    die1 = random.randint(1, 6)
    die2 = random.randint(1, 6)
    return die1 + die2

5.16.4. In this case we actually know the underlying probability of each sum#

These probabilities can be calculated theoretically by counting the number of ways each possible sum can occur when rolling two six-sided dice. Since each die has 6 faces, there are 6 \(\times\) 6 = 36 total possible outcomes.

Total Outcomes:
There are 36 total possible outcomes when rolling two six-sided dice.

Number of Ways to Achieve Each Sum:

  • Sum of 2: (1,1) — 1 way

  • Sum of 3: (1,2), (2,1) — 2 ways

  • Sum of 4: (1,3), (2,2), (3,1) — 3 ways

  • Sum of 5: (1,4), (2,3), (3,2), (4,1) — 4 ways

  • Sum of 6: (1,5), (2,4), (3,3), (4,2), (5,1) — 5 ways

  • Sum of 7: (1,6), (2,5), (3,4), (4,3), (5,2), (6,1) — 6 ways

  • Sum of 8: (2,6), (3,5), (4,4), (5,3), (6,2) — 5 ways

  • Sum of 9: (3,6), (4,5), (5,4), (6,3) — 4 ways

  • Sum of 10: (4,6), (5,5), (6,4) — 3 ways

  • Sum of 11: (5,6), (6,5) — 2 ways

  • Sum of 12: (6,6) — 1 way

Calculate Probabilities: The probability of each sum is the number of ways to achieve that sum divided by the total number of outcomes (36).

Let’s compute these probabilities:

Here are the theoretical probabilities for each possible sum when rolling two six-sided dice, calculated based on the number of ways each sum can be achieved:

  • Sum of 2: 1/36 \(\approx\) 0.028 (2.78%)

  • Sum of 3: 2/36 \(\approx\) 0.056 (5.56%)

  • Sum of 4: 3/36 \(\approx\) 0.083 (8.33%)

  • Sum of 5: 4/36 \(\approx\) 0.111 (11.11%)

  • Sum of 6: 5/36 \(\approx\) 0.139 (13.89%)

  • Sum of 7: 6/36 \(\approx\) 0.167 (16.67%)

  • Sum of 8: 5/36 \(\approx\) 0.139 (13.89%)

  • Sum of 9: 4/36 \(\approx\) 0.111 (11.11%)

  • Sum of 10: 4/36 \(\approx\) 0.083 (8.33%)

  • Sum of 11: 3/36 \(\approx\) 0.056 (5.56%)

  • Sum of 12: 1/36 \(\approx\) 0.028 (2.78%)

5.16.5. How does the measured probability of this event compare to the theoretical?#

# Count the frequency of each sum
sum_counts = {i: dice_sums.count(i) for i in range(2, 13)}

# Convert the counts into probabilities by dividing by the total number of rolls
total_rolls = len(dice_sums)
sum_probabilities = {sum_value: count / total_rolls for sum_value, count in sum_counts.items()}

# Theoretical probabilities for each sum
theoretical_probabilities = {
    2: 1/36,
    3: 2/36,
    4: 3/36,
    5: 4/36,
    6: 5/36,
    7: 6/36,
    8: 5/36,
    9: 4/36,
    10: 3/36,
    11: 2/36,
    12: 1/36
}

# Plotting the empirical probabilities as a bar chart
plt.bar(sum_probabilities.keys(), sum_probabilities.values(), edgecolor='black', label='Empirical')

# Plotting the theoretical probabilities as a line plot
plt.plot(theoretical_probabilities.keys(), theoretical_probabilities.values(), color='red', marker='o', linestyle='-', label='Theoretical')
plt.xlabel('Sum of Two Dice')
plt.ylabel('Probability')
plt.title('Probability Distribution of Dice Roll Sums')
plt.xticks(range(2, 13))  # Setting x-ticks to show possible sums
plt.legend()
plt.show()

So, we see that the empirical probabilities EXACTLY matches the measured probabilities when we take more and more measurements
